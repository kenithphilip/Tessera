# A security mesh for AI agents: honest review of Tessera and AgentMesh

Tessera+AgentMesh is **one of only two open-source projects in April 2026 that combines scanner breadth, a real policy plane, workload identity, and tamper-evident audit into a deployable security mesh for LLM agents** — the other being Microsoft's Agent Governance Toolkit, announced two weeks ago. The combination of signed trust labels with taint tracking, schema-enforced dual-LLM, SPIFFE/SPIRE identity, OPA/CEL policy, and SARIF+NIST compliance exports occupies a genuine white space that no single commercial vendor fully matches today. But the project ships at an awkward moment: the category is being rapidly absorbed into platform bundles (Palo Alto Networks bought Protect AI for ~$500M; Cisco acquired Robust Intelligence; SentinelOne took Prompt Security; F5 got CalypsoAI; Cato absorbed Aim Security), and the most architecturally similar OSS rival — **Solo.io's agentgateway, now Linux Foundation v1.0 with AWS/Cisco/IBM/Microsoft/Red Hat contributing** — has 18 months of runway, a Rust data plane, and the neutrality a sole-maintainer AGPL project cannot match. The project's 59% AgentDojo APR is respectable but well behind state-of-the-art (CaMeL ~100% blocking with 77% task completion; LlamaFirewall ~98% at the tuned operating point). The AGPL license on the **library** (Tessera) is the single biggest self-inflicted adoption wound. This report argues Tessera's best path forward is not to win the mesh layer, but to become the **policy and identity module that agentgateway, LiteLLM, and Microsoft's toolkit all call** — while monetizing through managed compliance tooling and threat intelligence rather than proxy licensing.

## Where the project actually sits

Tessera is a 26,800-line, 98-module, AGPL-3.0 Python library with a Rust workspace under way and a 1.4ms-overhead FastAPI proxy (AgentMesh) exposing 39 endpoints and 15 SDK adapters. Its two load-bearing invariants — **HMAC-signed trust labels with `allow(tool, ctx) iff required_trust(tool) ≤ min_s s.trust_level`** taint monotonicity, and **Pydantic-schema-enforced Worker output** that closes Simon Willison's dual-LLM convention hole — are both genuine technical contributions. HMAC-signed taint labels specifically are absent from CaMeL, FIDES, and every commercial competitor surveyed; schema enforcement is a simpler, more portable reformulation of CaMeL's Python-AST-interpreter dual-LLM without needing a custom interpreter. The claimed ~50 μs per check versus CaMeL's ~13 μs is an honest tradeoff: Tessera pays a 4× HMAC premium for integrity of the labels themselves, which is defensible in a distributed multi-agent deployment where you cannot assume trusted internal propagation.

Against the AgentDojo leaderboard, Tessera's **59% Attack Prevention Rate with a claimed 100% utility** sits in a mid-tier band. CaMeL v2 achieves ~100% provable blocking with 77% task completion (vs 84% undefended baseline); LlamaFirewall (PromptGuard 2 + AlignmentCheck) reduces attack success from 17.6% → 1.7%; PromptArmor and MELON approach 0% ASR; FIDES blocks all 949 AgentDojo attacks under a two-policy lattice; Tool Filter gets to 7.5% ASR but drops utility to 53%. Tessera's strongest result is the **utility preservation claim**, which if it survives scrutiny is better than any structural defense on the leaderboard — but it deserves scrutiny, because structural defenses typically lose 5–10% even on benign tasks where untrusted data must legitimately drive action parameters (e.g., "summarize this email and send it to Alice" cannot easily be done via purely structured Worker fields). The Travel suite's 30% APR, buried in the project's own `GAP_ANALYSIS_AGENTDOJO.md`, is consistent with this: multi-step planning with free-form content is where schema-only enforcement leaks.

## Technical analysis against academic research

The academic picture in April 2026 is clear: **no single defense works, defense-in-depth is mandatory, and structural/information-flow-control defenses provide the strongest guarantees at usability cost**. Tessera implements several canonical patterns from the literature well, and ignores others that it should integrate.

**CaMeL** (Debenedetti et al., arXiv:2503.18813, Google DeepMind) is Tessera's closest relative. CaMeL's **Capabilities with Sources/Readers** give per-value provenance — who produced this datum, who is allowed to read it — which is strictly more expressive than Tessera's scalar `min(trust_level)`. Tessera's monotone-floor rule will over-restrict in cases where a specific piece of untrusted data is safe for a specific tool but not others, and may under-restrict when multiple readers need different guarantees. **Recommended improvement:** evolve Tessera's TrustLabel from `(level, hmac)` to `(level, sources[], readers[], hmac)` to match CaMeL's lattice without adopting its AST interpreter. This is a backward-compatible extension.

**FIDES** (Costa et al., arXiv:2505.23643, Microsoft, May 2025) runs closer to Tessera's architecture: deterministic information-flow control with label propagation on every variable, a lattice with integrity and secrecy axes, and **two global policies** (weak secrecy, integrity) rather than per-tool policies. FIDES specifically introduces **information capacity** — a one-bit channel (e.g., a Boolean) cannot carry an injection payload and can therefore be declassified. This is precisely the insight behind Tessera's Pydantic-schema Worker, but Tessera doesn't make it explicit. **Recommended improvement:** formalize Tessera's schema enforcement as a bounded-information-capacity declassification rule, which would let Tessera make formal-security claims in a FIDES-style argument.

**PCAS** (arXiv:2602.16708, Microsoft) reports **compliance moving from 48% to 93% with zero policy violations** on τ²-bench customer-service tasks by compiling declarative Datalog policies into a reference monitor over a fine-grained dependency graph. This is the direction Tessera's `policy_builder` should evolve toward: a real compiler from declarative intent to runtime monitor, not just deterministic+LLM-driven rule templates.

**LlamaFirewall** (Chennabasappa et al., arXiv:2505.03574, Meta, May 2025) is orthogonal. **PromptGuard 2** (86M/22M DeBERTa, 97.5% detection at 1% FPR) and **AlignmentCheck** (Llama-4-Maverick CoT auditor, 83% detection at 2.5% FPR on proprietary data) stack cleanly with structural defenses. Tessera already has a PromptGuard adapter — the gap is **AlignmentCheck's goal-hijack detection**, which no current Tessera scanner replicates. An `alignment_check` scanner that compares declared user intent to the sequence of tool calls would close a known gap exposed by AgentDojo's Travel and Workspace suites. Caveat: recent work (arXiv:2510.17276) shows multi-objective GCG can simultaneously evade PromptGuard 2 and AlignmentCheck, so this is defense-in-depth, not a terminator.

**Spotlighting** (Hines et al., arXiv:2403.14720, Microsoft) is largely subsumed by Tessera's stronger structural enforcement. Microsoft itself dropped spotlighting from Phase 2 of LLMail-Inject in favor of defensive system prompts because multilingual and space-less-token bypasses defeated datamarking. Tessera should not invest here.

**ASIDE** (Zverev et al., arXiv:2503.10566, ICLR 2025 Workshop) applies an isoclinic orthogonal rotation to data-token embeddings, architecturally separating instructions from data inside the model. This is model-level; Tessera is system-level. Complementary — if a customer runs an ASIDE-finetuned model, Tessera's Worker LLM is even harder to hijack.

**SecAlign** (Chen et al., arXiv:2410.05451, Meta, CCS 2025) uses DPO on injection triples and achieves <10% ASR against unseen attacks, reducing GCG ASR 4× over **StruQ**. Tessera should publish a "recommended Worker model" list that includes SecAlign/Meta-SecAlign fine-tunes, turning model choice into a configurable defense layer.

**AgentDojo** (Debenedetti et al., arXiv:2406.13352, ETH Zurich, NeurIPS 2024) is the benchmark that matters. Tessera has a **replay evaluation**, not a live one. This is a real credibility gap. The project should run against the live leaderboard with a named LLM (GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet) and publish full UA/targeted-ASR numbers per suite with the specific attack configuration (e.g., "Important Instructions" attack). A leaderboard submission at state-of-the-art would be the single biggest credibility unlock.

**AgentHarm** (Andriushchenko et al., arXiv:2410.09024, UK AISI + Gray Swan, ICLR 2025) tests a different threat model — malicious *user* with legitimate privilege, not injection — and Tessera's architecture doesn't address it because labels trust the user by construction. This is an honest scope limitation. Document it; don't try to fake coverage.

**PoisonedRAG** (Zou et al., arXiv:2402.07867, USENIX Security 2025) shows 90%+ ASR with five malicious documents per query. Tessera's `rag_guard` partially addresses this by tagging retrieved docs low-trust, but doesn't address the **retrieval manipulation** itself. Certifiably-robust-RAG techniques (arXiv:2405.15556) aren't integrated.

**Tool poisoning and MCP rug-pulls** (Invariant Labs, April 2025) are Tessera's best-aligned territory: the `tool_shadow` (Levenshtein) scanner, MCP baseline drift detector, and supply-chain (typosquat/homoglyph) scanner directly target these. **MCPTox** (arXiv:2508.14925), **MCP-SafetyBench** (arXiv:2512.15163), and **MCP Safety Audit** (arXiv:2504.03767) should be in Tessera's CI — running Tessera against all three benchmarks and publishing results is a demonstrable differentiator commercial competitors won't replicate quickly.

**Constitutional AI** (Bai et al., arXiv:2212.08073) is a training-time alignment technique, not an agent-security primitive. Claude 3.5 Sonnet being AgentDojo's most injection-resistant frontier model is the only empirically relevant connection. Tessera should continue treating model choice as a recommendation, not a defense layer.

## How Tessera stacks up against commercial products

The 2024–2026 acquisition wave has redefined the competitive picture: **Protect AI → Palo Alto Prisma AIRS (~$500M), Robust Intelligence → Cisco AI Defense (2024), Prompt Security → SentinelOne (2025), CalypsoAI → F5 (~$180M), Aim Security → Cato Networks (~$350M), Pangea → CrowdStrike (2025)**. Independent players remaining include **Noma Security ($132M total, $100M Series B July 2025)**, **HiddenLayer** (Missile Defense Agency SHIELD IDIQ awardee, airgapped platform), **Lakera** (Series A ~$20M, Gandalf attack corpus 50M+ data points), **Lasso Security**, **Zenity** ($55M+, 2025 Gartner Cool Vendor in Agentic AI TRiSM), and **WitnessAI** ($85.5M total, Jan 2026 Strategic round).

Tessera's **signed trust labels with taint propagation, SPIFFE/SPIRE workload identity, hash-chained audit logs with HMAC truncation seal, schema-enforced dual-LLM, and OPA/CEL programmable policy** constitute a combination **no surveyed commercial vendor fully matches**. The closest analogs in each dimension are: **Pangea's Secure Audit Log** (tamper-proof but not hash-chained), **Credal AI's permission mirroring** (derives agent perms from user identity — a different mechanism), **Zenity's intent-aware runtime analysis** (execution-path heuristics rather than signed labels), and **Arthur Shield's task-scoped rules** (static rule scoping, not propagating taint). None of the 22 commercial products reviewed implement SPIFFE-based workload identity for AI agents. None productize the dual-LLM pattern. The open-source AGPL delivery against an increasingly-proprietary-platform competitive set is **Tessera's strongest positioning vector**.

Where Tessera is clearly behind: **enterprise shadow-AI discovery** (Zenity, Aim, HiddenLayer, WitnessAI all have stronger coverage of SaaS copilots — Microsoft 365 Copilot, Salesforce Agentforce — that Tessera's SDK-only model can't see), **proprietary attack-data moats** (Lakera's Gandalf corpus, CalypsoAI's 10K/month attack generation, Protect AI's Recon suite — an OSS project needs a community-contributed adversarial corpus like Garak's as the credible equivalent), **model supply-chain scanning** (Protect AI Guardian and HiddenLayer AI Supply Chain are narrowly superior), and **federal accreditations** (HiddenLayer FedRAMP/DoD, Cisco AI Defense, Arthur DoD). Tessera's SARIF + NIST 800-53 exports help on paper but are not a substitute for a FedRAMP authorization.

Against the OSS competitors — **Guardrails AI (Hub, ~100 Apache-licensed validators), NeMo Guardrails (NVIDIA, Colang DSL), LLM Guard (Protect AI, 35 scanners, MIT), Rebuff (archived May 2025), Vigil-LLM (Adam Swanda, now Cisco), Promptfoo (OpenAI-acquired, eval/red-team), Giskard (testing), Garak (NVIDIA red-team)** — **Tessera is the only OSS project with a control plane** (policy + identity + audit). Everyone else is a library or a tester. This is defensible positioning, but the **number of scanner modules is not the moat** — LLM Guard has 35, Tessera has 98, but detector quality per module (measured by FPR on directive.py, intent.py, heuristic.py — flagged in the project's own gap analysis) is the real question, not count. Publishing scanner-by-scanner precision/recall numbers against Garak probes would separate Tessera from marketing-driven competitors.

## Infrastructure-layer competitors and the agentgateway reality

On the infrastructure side, the most important fact is **agentgateway v1.0.0, released 12 March 2026** by Solo.io under the Linux Foundation / Agentic AI Foundation, explicitly positioned as "Istio/Cilium for agentic AI." It is a **pure Rust data plane** (not Envoy — Solo concluded Envoy cannot cleanly handle MCP's stateful JSON-RPC/SSE/Streamable-HTTP sessions), Kubernetes Gateway API conformant, xDS-configured, with built-in LLM gateway, MCP gateway with OAuth 2.0 On-Behalf-Of flows, A2A gateway, and Gateway API Inference Extension support. Backers include **AWS, Cisco, Huawei, IBM, Microsoft, Red Hat, Shell, Zayo, Akamai, CoreWeave**. 115+ contributors, 1M+ image pulls in the last quarter.

**This is existential for AgentMesh's proxy positioning.** A FastAPI proxy cannot beat a Rust proxy backed by that coalition on performance, adoption, or enterprise trust. AgentMesh's ~1.4ms p50 overhead is in the same ballpark as agentgateway, but the ecosystem asymmetry is decisive. The right strategic move is **to upstream SPIFFE identity and policy modules to agentgateway** rather than try to compete on the data plane. Agentgateway explicitly does not ship a SPIFFE issuer and relies on external OAuth IdPs — this is the gap Tessera's identity work fills. Tessera's OPA/CEL policy engine, hash-chained audit, and MCP-drift scanners are also clear contribution candidates. A Rust `tessera-rs` PyO3 wheel already exists; porting Tessera's core primitives into an agentgateway plugin is a 6–12 month technical lift, not a rewrite.

**LLM gateways overlap on the routing layer**: Portkey AI has pivoted hard into governance (March 2026 Agent Gateway, 60+ guardrails, integrations with Prisma AIRS/Akto/Pillar/Patronus, pricing on recorded logs: Dev free, Pro $49–99/mo, Enterprise quote-based), LiteLLM (MIT, ~16K GitHub stars, widely deployed inside ML platforms, has a `CustomGuardrail` callback subsystem that's an obvious Tessera plug-point), Cloudflare AI Gateway (edge routing, thin security), Kong AI Gateway (3.12+, enterprise-mature MCP gateway), Vercel AI Gateway, OpenRouter. **None offer workload identity or propagating taint labels.** Tessera/AgentMesh should **not** try to replace LiteLLM or Portkey — both have too much distribution. Shipping first-class LiteLLM `CustomGuardrail` adapters and Portkey webhook integrations is the higher-leverage move.

**Observability is pure complement**: Langfuse (MIT, ~25K stars, acquired by ClickHouse January 2026), Helicone (Apache 2.0), LangSmith (proprietary, LangChain's $125M Series B product), Arize/Phoenix (Elastic License 2.0 OSS), Weave (W&B), Traceloop/OpenLLMetry (reference OTel GenAI SIG implementation), Datadog LLM Observability (the obvious SIEM partner, launched AI Agent Monitoring GA at DASH 2025). Tessera should emit **OpenTelemetry GenAI semantic conventions v0.5.x** natively — this alone is the interop layer that makes its audit data consumable by every one of these without custom code.

**On MCP specifically**: the current spec is **2025-11-25**, now governed by the Agentic AI Foundation under the Linux Foundation; MCP servers are OAuth 2.1 resource servers with mandatory **RFC 8707 Resource Indicators** audience binding, **RFC 9728 Protected Resource Metadata**, and Client ID Metadata Documents as the preferred registration mechanism. **SEP-1913 is a draft pull request in the `modelcontextprotocol` repo** authored by Sam Morrow, proposing **trust and sensitivity annotations** on MCP request/response data — markers like `untrustedPublic` and sensitivity levels. The user's task description framed this as an IETF draft; that's a conflation — SEP-1913 is not at IETF. What *is* at IETF is **draft-klrc-aiagent-auth-01 (30 March 2026)** in the WIMSE working group, authored by Kasselman (Defakto), Lombardo (AWS), Rosomakho (Zscaler), Campbell (Ping Identity), and Steele (OpenAI), proposing an Agent Identity Management System that composes SPIFFE, WIMSE IDs, Workload Identity Tokens, and OAuth Transaction Tokens. **The opportunity for Tessera is to contribute to SEP-1913 as a reference implementation** (trust labels ≈ MCP trust annotations) and to **ship WIMSE+klrc-aiagent-auth compliance as a differentiator** against Microsoft's toolkit, which doesn't yet claim WIMSE alignment.

## Standards alignment and compliance posture

The compliance landscape crystallized sharply in 2025–2026. **OWASP Top 10 for Agentic Applications 2026** was published 9 December 2025 (ASI01–ASI10: Goal Hijack, Tool Misuse, Identity/Privilege Abuse, Agentic Supply Chain, Unexpected RCE, Memory/Context Poisoning, Insecure Inter-Agent Comms, Cascading Failures, Human-Agent Trust Exploitation, Rogue Agents) — this is now the primary enterprise taxonomy and Tessera's SARIF should emit `ASI##` tags natively alongside its existing CWE and 800-53 enrichment. **OWASP LLM Top 10 v2:2025** (LLM01 Prompt Injection through LLM10 Unbounded Consumption) remains the LLM-specific reference. **MITRE ATLAS v5.4.0 (February 2026)** expanded to 16 tactics, 84 techniques, 42 case studies — including agent-specific techniques (Context Poisoning, Memory Manipulation, Modify AI Agent Configuration, Exfiltration via AI Agent Tool Invocation, Publish Poisoned AI Agent Tool). Publishing a **MITRE ATLAS Navigator layer** showing Tessera's technique coverage is table stakes for SOC buyers.

**NIST AI RMF 1.0** (Govern/Map/Measure/Manage with numbered subcategories) is the de facto US federal expectation; **NIST AI 600-1 (July 2024)** enumerates 12 GenAI risks that map directly onto detector categories; **NIST SP 800-218A (July 2024)** extends the SSDF for generative AI. The **Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI)** — the renamed US AISI — issued an RFI on securing agentic AI systems in January 2026, and in February 2026 launched the **AI Agent Standards Initiative** and an NCCoE concept paper on AI agent identity and authorization centered on **WIMSE/SPIFFE + OAuth with policy-based access control**. This is an extraordinary alignment signal for Tessera — its architecture **literally matches the NCCoE concept**. The project should volunteer Tessera as an NCCoE practice-guide reference implementation. That's a seal of legitimacy no commercial competitor can match under the current US deregulatory posture.

The **EU AI Act** (Regulation 2024/1689) has **2 August 2026 as the critical full-obligation date** for Annex III high-risk systems (Art. 9 risk management, Art. 12 record-keeping, Art. 14 human oversight, Art. 15 cybersecurity/robustness). GPAI obligations (Art. 51–55) are already in force; the **EU GPAI Code of Practice (10 July 2025)**, chaired on Safety & Security by Yoshua Bengio, explicitly addresses agentic use in Measure 5.1. Tessera's audit log is directly evidentiary for Art. 12, and its policy engine for Art. 9 and 15. **ISO/IEC 42001:2023** Annex A controls (9 objectives, 38 controls) map cleanly onto Tessera's runtime evidence; **CSA AICM 1.0 (July 2025)** with 243 control objectives and the new **STAR for AI** certification (October 2025) is a high-value target because producing an AI-CAIQ response is becoming a procurement artifact.

**Microsoft's Agent Governance Toolkit (April 2026)** is the competitive threat here: MIT-licensed, seven packages including an **Agent Mesh** sub-package (name collision with AgentMesh — a real branding problem), claiming first coverage of all 10 OWASP Agentic risks with deterministic enforcement, multi-language (Python, TypeScript, Rust, Go, .NET), integrations with LangChain, CrewAI, Google ADK, Microsoft Agent Framework, OpenAI Agents SDK, LangGraph, Haystack, PydanticAI, LlamaIndex, Dify, and Microsoft has signaled foundation donation. **Tessera's response:** explicit naming clarification; WIMSE/SPIFFE as a differentiator (Microsoft's toolkit doesn't claim it); framework-adapter parity (Tessera already has 14+, so leverage this); and the honest observation that Microsoft's "sub-ms policy engine with p99 <0.1 ms" is Rust-native performance Tessera's FastAPI cannot match — another argument for porting the critical path to Rust.

## The honest gap list

The project's own `GAP_ANALYSIS_AGENTDOJO.md` already names the biggest problems; this report amplifies them in priority order.

**Priority 1 — Missing AlignmentCheck-equivalent scanner.** The AgentDojo Travel suite at 30% APR almost certainly reflects goal-hijack attacks that schema enforcement cannot catch because the Worker produces validly-structured fields with attacker-chosen values. LlamaFirewall's AlignmentCheck pattern — comparing declared intent to the tool-call sequence — is the canonical mitigation. An LLM-as-judge scanner comparing `user_intent` to `tool_call_chain` at plan-verifier level would likely move Banking, Travel, and Workspace APRs materially.

**Priority 2 — Live AgentDojo leaderboard evaluation, not replay.** The 59% APR replay number is suspect to external reviewers because it's not reproducible on the live harness. Submitting to `agentdojo.spylab.ai` with GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet, publishing full UA/targeted-ASR per suite with the specific attack config, is the single most credible one-week task.

**Priority 3 — Evolve TrustLabel from scalar to CaMeL-style capabilities.** `(level, sources[], readers[], hmac)` instead of `(level, hmac)` gives per-reader and per-source expressiveness without abandoning the HMAC integrity property. Backward compatible.

**Priority 4 — Production runtime isolation.** Tessera's own OWASP/NIST control matrix notes runtime isolation and network egress enforcement are not enforced. The three-tier architecture (Solo <0.5ms, Team <5ms, Enterprise <15ms with Firecracker + Tetragon) is designed but not shipped. Ship at least Team tier: Firecracker microVM execution for destructive tools + Tetragon/Cilium-based egress filtering. This is **ASI05 (Unexpected RCE) + ASI03 (Identity/Privilege Abuse)** coverage in the new OWASP Agentic Top 10 — unambiguously enterprise-procurement blocking.

**Priority 5 — Scanner precision reporting.** Publish FPR/TPR for `directive.py`, `intent.py`, `heuristic.py` against a public adversarial corpus (Garak probes, MCPTox, InjecAgent). The 98-scanner count is meaningless without calibration; any competitor with 35 well-tuned scanners beats 98 noisy ones.

**Priority 6 — Rust data plane productionization.** The `tessera-core/scanners/audit/policy/runtime/gateway/bench/py` workspace exists at v0.9.0-alpha.1 but the FastAPI proxy is the reference deployment. With agentgateway's Rust v1.0 out and Microsoft's toolkit shipping Rust at sub-ms p99, the FastAPI proxy is a performance ceiling. Elevate the Rust workspace to production, keep the FastAPI as a reference SDK.

**Priority 7 — Supply-chain integrity for prompts and tools.** Sign and version-pin system prompts, tool descriptions, and MCP manifests. Detect rug-pull attacks (Invariant Labs April 2025) and the recent `mcp-remote` CVE-2025-6514 class. Tessera's `tool_shadow` and MCP drift scanners are a start; cryptographic pinning is the complete answer.

**Priority 8 — Newer attack classes.** Memory poisoning (addressed in ASI06 and Gemini memory attack case study), multi-turn attacks (GOAT, Best-of-N), "lethal trifecta" combinations (tool-poison + rug-pull + rogue agent). Current scanners are largely single-turn; a session-stateful scanner tracking cross-turn anomaly is missing. The v0.7.1 session-isolation fix hints at more lurking — a systematic threat-model review by an external reviewer (NCC Group, Trail of Bits, Doyensec) before a v1.0 release is cheap insurance.

**Priority 9 — Operational story.** No SPIRE in CI; no production deployment beyond docker-compose; no Helm chart for Kubernetes; no air-gapped install docs. Every enterprise buyer will ask for these. Ship them before the first five-figure contract.

**Priority 10 — AGPL on the library.** Covered in the commercialization section — this is a strategic, not technical, gap, but it *blocks* technical adoption.

## Commercialization strategy

The stated goal is sustainability, not revenue maximization. That should shape every pricing decision toward **adoption-maximizing over monetization-maximizing** — but with enough monetization to fund 1–3 full-time developers, which means roughly **$300K–$500K ARR by year 2, $1M+ by year 3**.

**The single most important license decision: change Tessera (the library) to Apache 2.0 or MPL 2.0; keep AgentMesh (the server) as AGPL-3.0-or-later.** The FSF interpretation that dynamically linking AGPL code is a derivative work makes an AGPL Python library **effectively unusable** by any enterprise that embeds it in a proprietary application — which is most of the target market. Grafana does exactly this split (Apache plugins/agents, AGPL server); MongoDB's AGPL era failed partly because AGPL-on-the-library was the adoption wound. Adopt a CLA or DCO with copyright assignment **from day one** to preserve the right to dual-license or re-license later; retrofitting after external contributions arrive is nearly impossible.

**The four-tier pricing structure the data supports:**

| Tier | Price | Target | Key inclusion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Community / self-host | Free (Apache Tessera + AGPL AgentMesh) | Solo devs, pre-seed startups | Full functionality, community support |
| Cloud Starter | $29/mo | Indie devs, early startups | 100K guarded requests, 5 agents, 30-day retention, unlimited users |
| Team / Pro | $199/mo base + usage | Growing startups, AI infra builders | 1M requests, 90-day retention, audit export, basic SSO, SOC2 Type II, Datadog export |
| Enterprise | From $25K/yr | F500, regulated verticals | SSO/SAML/SCIM, on-prem/air-gapped, HIPAA BAA, SIEM integrations, policy-as-code, named SLA, threat intel briefings |

These numbers track Langfuse (Core $29, Pro $199, Enterprise $2,499), Sentry ($26 Team → $80 Business → custom), Grafana ($25K/yr Enterprise minimum), and Supabase ($25 Pro → $599 Team). **Unlimited users should be standard from the $29 tier** — the emerging PostHog/Langfuse norm and a strong differentiator over per-seat competitors.

**Primary pricing axis: protected agents + guarded requests hybrid.** Per-seat is wrong for security (punishes wide rollout). Per-token is opaque and wrongly maps security value to LLM cost. Per-request is natural at the proxy layer. An "AI agent" is a natural unit customers already think in (Temporal's Actions, Grafana's active series).

**Target segment recommendations:**

*AI-native startups (primary early target).* Self-serve, credit card, <$100/mo. Docs-first PLG motion. Realistic 0.3–1% GitHub-stars-to-paid conversion (Plausible's benchmark was ~7K paying subs on ~15K stars). Goal: 200–500 paying Starter/Team accounts in year 2 = $60K–$300K ARR.

*AI infrastructure providers (Portkey, Vercel AI, Cloudflare AI Gateway).* These are partners first, customers second. Ship LiteLLM `CustomGuardrail`, Portkey webhook, Kong plugin, agentgateway module adapters. OEM licensing or co-marketing is higher-value than trying to sell them the product. Recommend: two or three partnership deals at $25K–$75K/yr each for co-branded editions.

*Enterprise F500 AI teams.* Sales-led, $75K–$300K/yr initial contracts, 3–12 month evaluations requiring SOC 2 Type II, SSO/SCIM, on-prem option, custom SLA, indemnification. The project is 12–18 months of compliance investment away from this segment (SOC 2 Type II: ~$30K–$100K plus ~$500K annual operational cost; HIPAA BAA; FedRAMP-in-process listing). Don't target this segment in year 1 except through design partners.

*Foundation model providers (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, xAI, Mistral).* Fewer than 50 companies globally; they build security in-house. Not a viable sales segment. Possible: red-team-as-a-service or eval data licensing if the community builds a Garak-equivalent around Tessera.

*AI IDE builders (Cursor at $500M ARR, Windsurf, Zed, JetBrains AI, Replit, Lovable at $100M ARR).* High-growth segment that will need a security layer as they take enterprise deals. OEM-style licensing $25K–$150K/yr per IDE vendor, bundled into their enterprise SKU. Tessera's existing Claude Code / Cursor / Copilot CLI / Gemini CLI adapters are the entry wedge. **This is arguably the highest-leverage early enterprise segment** because the IDE vendors' own enterprise motions create downstream pull.

*Vertical AI (fintech, healthcare, legal).* Highest willingness to pay for compliance-gated features. Requires certifications Tessera doesn't have yet. Strategy: ride partner SOC2 (Langfuse/Portkey) in year 1, pursue own HIPAA BAA + SOC 2 Type II in year 2, price $150K–$500K/yr per customer.

**Features that should be pricing triggers** (in order of willingness-to-pay signal strength): SSO/SAML/SCIM (universal enterprise gate, keep markup ethical at 2–3× Pro, not 10×); on-premise / air-gapped deployment (Mattermost / Vault / HiddenLayer pattern, premium 3–10× over cloud); HIPAA BAA / FedRAMP; managed SPIRE infrastructure (real differentiator); SIEM integrations (Splunk, Datadog, Panther, QRadar); threat intelligence feeds (known bad MCP servers, injection patterns — this is the closest analog to Lakera's data moat that an OSS project can build, and a natural Enterprise-tier feature); compliance exports (SOC 2, EU AI Act evidence bundles — Tessera already has the raw material); red-team-as-a-service; LLM guardrail fine-tuning on customer data; dedicated model for policy synthesis.

**AGPL does meaningfully deter SaaS cloning** (forces open-sourcing of modifications) but **doesn't prevent API reimplementation** (MongoDB's lesson with AWS DocumentDB). Build the moat in **detector models, threat intelligence, and integrations** — not in the proxy code. Accept that the proxy itself will be commoditized by agentgateway and Microsoft's toolkit.

## Strategic integration and ecosystem positioning

The highest-leverage strategic move is **not** winning the mesh layer. It's becoming **the policy+identity+audit module that every other layer calls**. Five specific plays:

**Upstream to agentgateway.** Contribute SPIFFE-based SVID validation, hash-chained audit event sink, OPA/CEL policy integration, and MCP-drift scanners as Rust plugins. This preserves Tessera's technical differentiation while riding the Linux Foundation coalition. Agentgateway maintainers are explicit that they don't ship a SPIFFE issuer and rely on OPA/Kyverno/OpenFGA for ABAC — the contribution surface is defined.

**Contribute to MCP SEP-1913.** Sam Morrow's draft on trust and sensitivity annotations is under-specified in ways Tessera already has working code for. Become the reference implementation. If SEP-1913 is accepted, Tessera's trust labels become the canonical way to express MCP trust annotations in the ecosystem — a category-defining win with minimal investment.

**Become default security in LangChain / LangGraph / CrewAI / LlamaIndex / PydanticAI middleware.** The 14+ adapters are already there. Work with LangChain's LangSmith team (post-$125M Series B) and NeMo Guardrails maintainers to establish Tessera as a recommended callback/middleware in documentation. This is non-exclusive and free to execute.

**Partner with model providers for provenance signing.** Anthropic (RSP v3.1, ASL-3 models), OpenAI (Agents SDK), Google (ADK, SAIF donated to CoSAI September 2025), AWS (Agentic AI Scoping Matrix, four agentic security principles January 2026). Each has a provenance-signing angle. Tessera's HMAC-signed trust labels could become the transport for model-attested provenance.

**OTel GenAI semantic conventions compliance.** Emit `gen_ai.*` spans natively; the convention covers `execute_tool`, `create_agent`, `invoke_agent`, `gen_ai.tool.call.arguments`, `gen_ai.conversation.id`. This is the single cheapest interop layer that makes Tessera data consumable by Langfuse, LangSmith, Phoenix, Weave, Datadog, and every future observability backend without custom work.

## Honest critical assessment

**Is the architecture sound?** Yes, with caveats. The two load-bearing invariants — HMAC-signed trust labels with monotone-floor taint, and Pydantic-schema Worker enforcement — are technically defensible and align with the direction academic literature is moving (CaMeL, FIDES, PCAS). The scalar `min(trust_level)` is less expressive than CaMeL's capabilities and should evolve. The schema enforcement is a genuinely good simplification of the dual-LLM pattern that closes Willison's original convention hole. **The biggest blind spot is the threat model assumption that Worker structured output is sufficient to prevent argument-level poisoning** — a poisoned passage can still fill a valid `recipient_email` field with an attacker-chosen address. This is the gap CaMeL's capabilities and FIDES's integrity labels address and Tessera's scheme does not.

**Is the AGPL license helping or hurting?** Hurting, on the library. AGPL-on-the-library is the single most costly license choice an open-source project can make outside of source-available licenses. AGPL on the server (AgentMesh) is defensible and aligns with Grafana/Mattermost/Plausible precedent. Change Tessera to Apache 2.0 or MPL 2.0 before any major promotional push. This single change is likely worth more adoption than any technical feature.

**The #1 highest-leverage technical improvement:** **ship an AlignmentCheck-style goal-hijack scanner that compares declared user intent to the tool-call chain**, benchmark against live AgentDojo with a named LLM, and publish per-suite UA/ASR numbers. This lifts Tessera from "respectable mid-tier APR" to plausibly-best-in-class utility-weighted security, and makes the 59%→?% improvement the project's credibility moment.

**The #1 highest-leverage strategic move:** **propose Tessera as an NCCoE reference implementation for the February 2026 AI Agent Identity and Authorization practice guide**, and **upstream SPIFFE+policy modules to agentgateway**. The NCCoE move creates federal legitimacy no competitor can match. The agentgateway move converts an existential competitive threat into a strategic amplifier and locks in ecosystem neutrality the single-maintainer structure cannot achieve alone.

**Standards to align with or be orphaned:** OWASP Agentic Top 10 2026 (native SARIF tagging), MITRE ATLAS v5.4.0 (Navigator layer), NIST AI 600-1 twelve-risk tagging, EU AI Act Articles 9/12/14/15 citations, ISO/IEC 42001 Annex A, CSA AICM 1.0, draft-klrc-aiagent-auth (WIMSE), SPIFFE, OAuth 2.1 + RFC 8707 for MCP authorization, OpenTelemetry GenAI semantic conventions. The project is one focused sprint away from covering all of these in SARIF metadata.

**Can this compete with well-funded commercial products?** On technical merit, yes — the architectural combination is genuinely unique. On enterprise go-to-market, no: Palo Alto, Cisco, SentinelOne, F5, and Cato are bundling AI security into existing security subscriptions and absorbing the independent market. The realistic commercial ceiling for a solo-maintainer AGPL project in this category is **$3M–$10M ARR over 3–5 years**, which is enough to sustain 3–10 developers and fulfill the stated sustainability goal. It is not a venture outcome. Framing matters: Tessera's value is as **enduring open infrastructure** (like Traefik, Grafana, Temporal), not as a venture-scale SaaS.

**The 6 / 12 / 24 month priorities:**

*Next 6 months:* License split (Tessera Apache, AgentMesh AGPL) with CLA. Live AgentDojo submission. AlignmentCheck scanner. OpenTelemetry GenAI semconv export. OWASP Agentic Top 10 SARIF tagging. Helm chart and production deployment docs. First three LiteLLM/Portkey/agentgateway partnership adapters. Rust workspace to beta.

NCCoE reference implementation proposal. MCP SEP-1913 contribution as reference impl. SOC 2 Type I. First 3–5 Enterprise design-partner contracts at $50K–$100K each. Rust workspace to GA and first upstream contribution to agentgateway.

*Next 24 months:* SOC 2 Type II. HIPAA BAA. FedRAMP-in-process listing. Target $1M ARR. Community-driven Garak-equivalent red-team corpus. Policy-synthesis LLM as Enterprise-tier feature. Consider foundation governance to address the solo-maintainer bus factor and enable enterprise trust at scale.

## Bottom line

Tessera+AgentMesh is **better than most of its commercial competitors on the architectural primitives that will actually matter in three years** — signed trust labels, workload identity, dual-LLM schema enforcement, tamper-evident audit, programmable policy — and worse than most of them on the enterprise go-to-market fundamentals (proprietary threat data, certifications, sales motion, brand, and bus factor). The project has a real technical contribution and a genuine white space. It is shipping into a consolidating market with a well-funded open-source rival (agentgateway) and a fresh Microsoft entrant (Agent Governance Toolkit, with a colliding name) that each have 18 months of momentum Tessera cannot match head-on. The path that makes the technical contributions durable is **not winning the mesh, but becoming the policy, identity, and compliance layer every mesh calls** — which requires a license change, standards alignment, and ecosystem contributions more than it requires new features. If the author prioritizes the 6-month list above, Tessera becomes the Traefik of AI agent security: not the biggest name in the category, but the one everyone in the category depends on.
